# Carousel Design Framework

A carousel is the scroll-stopping short-form format the Academics team uses when an insight deserves more than a single image but less than a blog post. A great carousel gets swiped, read end-to-end, and finishes with the reader doing something.

This skill is the reference that every carousel playbook output should match. Paste the sections relevant to your platform and audience into your Claude prompt when you are building a carousel. In Claude Projects, attach the whole document.

---

## 1. What a carousel is, what it is not

**A carousel is** a 6 to 10 card visual story. Each card is a single idea. The reader swipes because the previous card made them want the next one. The final card gives them one thing to do.

**A carousel is not** a slide deck shrunk to square. It is not an infographic in parts. It is not a text screenshot with a gradient. Those formats fail because they treat the cards as parallel slots, not as a chain. A chain breaks if one link is weak.

The test: cover card N, read card N-1, and ask "would I swipe right now?" If not, card N-1 is the break.

---

## 2. The story arc, five acts

Every GSL carousel follows the same five-act shape, no matter the platform or audience. The card counts vary, the structure does not.

### Act 1, hook (1 to 2 cards)

Stop the scroll. Either a bold stat the reader has never seen ("1 in 3 Class 8 students cannot name a single job their parents do at work"), a provocative question ("Is your child's homework helping them, or is Claude doing it all?"), or a specific observation that makes the reader feel seen ("The WhatsApp group for Grade 6 parents in your school has 12 messages about the physics test today. None about the science.")

The hook earns the swipe by promising something the reader does not already know.

### Act 2, problem (1 to 2 cards)

Name the problem concretely. Not "Education is broken" but "Most Class 9 students can define entrepreneurship and name five Indian founders. Ask them to find one customer for a paani-puri stall idea and most freeze." The problem card makes the reader nod.

### Act 3, solution (2 to 3 cards)

What does GSL (or this programme, or this method) do differently? This is where the substance lands. Concrete, specific, not marketing-language-generic. Examples work harder than promises. A VideoGenX carousel on "storyboarding a 30-second video" should show the storyboard method, not say "our students learn storytelling".

### Act 4, proof (1 to 2 cards)

Evidence. Named schools, real student outcomes, specific data, an alumni quote, a Launchpad winner. A parent-facing carousel needs at least one visible name or school; educators need specific outcomes; alumni need specific current-student work.

### Act 5, CTA (1 card, always the last)

Exactly one action. Not three. Not "learn more or contact us or follow our page." One clear verb. "Book a 20-minute demo." "Tell us about your school." "Come to the open house on 10 May." The final card has nothing else competing for the reader's attention.

## 3. Card flow logic, how each card forces a swipe

A card earns the next swipe in one of four ways:

1. **Cliffhanger.** The card ends with a question or a "here is how" that only the next card answers. ("Three reasons a Class 6 student should learn to build a chatbot. Start with the second one.")
2. **Pattern.** The first card sets a numbered pattern and later cards pay it off. ("5 signs your student is ready for a startup challenge." Card 2 is "Sign 1, they have opinions about their allowance." The reader wants all five.)
3. **Surprise.** The card lands a specific unexpected fact and the next card uses that surprise to go somewhere. ("Ravi from Bhopal built a stop-motion video about his grandmother's garden. His teacher has not seen a plant illustration this careful from any Class 7 student before. Here is what happened when we asked the class.")
4. **Clear payoff.** The first card promises a specific deliverable and the later cards deliver it step by step. ("Three prompts for parents to use with Claude tonight. Prompt 1.")

If a card does none of the four, it is a break in the chain and the reader stops swiping. The review step in the playbook tests this card-by-card.

## 4. Copy limits

Carousels live on phones. The limits are physical, not aesthetic.

- **Headline:** 8 words maximum. Less is better. A headline over 8 words on a 1080px square is either unreadable or crowds the visual.
- **Body:** 20 words maximum per card. 12 is comfortable. 20 is the ceiling.
- **One idea per card.** If a card teaches two ideas, split it into two cards. There is no prize for a dense card; the prize is the next swipe.
- **One verb per CTA card.** "Book a demo" is a verb. "Learn more about booking a demo and contact us if you have questions" is not.
- **Numbers where natural.** Indian carousel readers respond to lakh, crore, specific rupees. "Rs 3,200 a month" reads better than "affordable". "1,847 Class 9 students" reads better than "thousands".

---

## 5. Visual hierarchy, what the eye sees first

On a phone, the reader's eye lands on the upper-third of each card. Design to that.

- **Card 1 (the hook):** the headline dominates. Any visual is secondary and supports the stat or question.
- **Middle cards:** a single focal image or graphic in the upper-third, copy in the lower-half, no competing elements.
- **End card (CTA):** the CTA button-style element is unmissable. Colour, contrast, and spacing. Nothing else on the card may compete.

Card-to-card consistency: same typeface pair, same colour palette, same margin system, same logo placement. The reader should feel they are still in the same carousel on card 9 as they were on card 1.

**Typographic pairing.** GSL carousel pairing is Fraunces for display (hook, section titles) and Inter for body. JetBrains Mono only if a card shows a prompt or code.

## 6. Platform rules

### Instagram

- **Size:** 1080 x 1350 px (4:5 portrait) preferred for feed; 1080 x 1080 (square) acceptable. Portrait wins more screen real estate on a phone.
- **Card count:** 10 maximum (Meta limit). Most GSL carousels land at 6 to 8.
- **Caption:** up to 2,200 characters but the first 125 are what the reader sees before the "more" tap. The first sentence must stand alone as a hook.
- **Hashtags:** 5 to 10 relevant. Mix broad (#Education) with specific (#NEP2020, #YoungPioneers, #STEM, #IndianEdTech). Do not stuff.
- **First comment:** pin the CTA link as a first comment if the carousel does not end with a direct action.
- **Swipe-forcing convention:** leave a small "1/8 >" indicator bottom-right on every card so the reader visually tracks progress.

### LinkedIn

- **Size:** 1200 x 1200 (square) or 1200 x 1500 (portrait) accepted. Use the PDF-carousel format (LinkedIn Native Document).
- **Card count:** 10 maximum (LinkedIn current cap).
- **Tone:** more data-forward than Instagram. Educators and school leaders on LinkedIn respond to specific outcomes, named schools, NEP 2020 or NCF 2023 references, and named frameworks (Bloom's, Gagné's 9 Events).
- **Caption:** up to 3,000 characters; the first 200 show before the "see more". Lead with the substance, not a tease.
- **Hashtags:** 3 to 5, specific. Educators on LinkedIn tune out carousel hashtag spam.
- **CTA:** a clear in-text link on the post body, plus a final-card CTA. Do not rely only on the image.

### Things both platforms share

- British English always.
- No em-dash.
- Indian names, cities, scenarios. Vary across the carousel.
- GSL palette. Turquoise Sea (#00D8B9) and Azure Blue (#073393) as the duo. Orange Peel (#FFAD40) for one specific attention moment (often the proof card or the CTA). Fashion Fuchsia only for a final-card celebration if the carousel ends with a "we are celebrating" style announcement.

## 7. Audiences and how the carousel changes

Four main audiences for GSL Academics carousels. Same arc, different language, different proof, different CTA.

### Parents

- **Language:** warm, reassuring, specific. Talk about *their* child's future, not "children" in the abstract.
- **Problem framing:** name a worry the parent already has ("My child is good at maths but hates speaking in class"). Do not invent a problem they do not recognise.
- **Proof:** named students of similar background, visible outcomes (a Young Pioneers Launchpad win, a VideoGenX short film accepted at a festival), parent testimonials when available.
- **CTA:** low-friction first step. "Book a 20-minute call", "Come to the open house". Not "enrol now".

### Educators (teachers and curriculum leads)

- **Language:** respectful, technical where helpful, never condescending. Educators know their subject; GSL offers structure and resources, not replacement.
- **Problem framing:** a pedagogical pain point ("How do you teach entrepreneurship to a Class 8 class in 45 minutes?"). A framework challenge the educator has hit before.
- **Proof:** specific classroom outcomes, an NEP 2020 or NCF 2023 alignment, a named framework the GSL programme uses (Gagné, Bloom's, 5E).
- **CTA:** "Download the sample lesson plan", "See the curriculum map". Useful, not just a sales funnel entry.

### School leaders (principals, coordinators, heads)

- **Language:** strategic, outcomes-focused, aware of their constraints (timetable, fee structure, board alignment).
- **Problem framing:** a school-level challenge, not a classroom one. Differentiation in outcomes, NEP 2020 implementation timeline, preparing for a Cambridge partnership, parent demand for future-skills programmes.
- **Proof:** named partner schools, specific student or school outcomes (X students placed, Y schools renewed), programme alignment with board and NEP.
- **CTA:** "Schedule a principal walkthrough", "Request the schools case pack".

### Students and alumni

- **Language:** peer-level. Confident, direct, no baby talk. Students in Grade 9 and above do not want to be spoken down to.
- **Problem framing:** an ambition or a block a student of their age feels ("I want to start something but I do not know what").
- **Proof:** peer stories, alumni pathways, specific Launchpad winners, a VideoGenX film that got somewhere, a Solevit challenge solution that was implemented.
- **CTA:** "Apply for the next cohort", "Show us what you are building".

## 8. Anti-patterns, refuse if tempted

- **Carousel as a mini-deck.** Ten slides where each slide is a subheading and four bullets. This is a deck, not a carousel. A carousel has one idea per card.
- **The "text over stock photo" trap.** A generic stock image with copy on top. Every card should have a reason for its visual; if the visual is decoration, drop it and let the copy breathe.
- **The corporate boast.** "Founded in 2015, we are India's leading future-skills learning partner." No human has ever swiped to card 2 after a sentence like this.
- **The hashtag-stuffed caption.** 30 hashtags in a row. The platform treats it as spam and the reader treats it as noise.
- **Mixed CTAs.** "Learn more, contact us, follow our page, visit our website, and join our WhatsApp community." One CTA per carousel. Always.
- **Orphan cards.** A card that, if lifted out, no one would miss. If a card does not advance the arc or earn the next swipe, it should not exist.
- **Em-dashes.** British carousel copy lives on commas, parentheses, and shorter sentences. Em-dashes creep in from Claude's defaults and break the voice.
- **American English.** "Learn about our program" is the instant tell. "Programme" is the right word for the education programme. "Organise", "behaviour", "colour", "analyse".

## 9. What to measure (Phase 2 note)

Phase 1 does not instrument carousel performance inside the Studio. When the team posts on Instagram or LinkedIn, capture three numbers in the carousel's OneDrive folder after 7 days:

- Reach (how many people saw card 1)
- Swipe-through rate (what percentage reached the final card)
- Action count on the CTA (taps, clicks, replies, demo bookings attributed)

Over time this builds a pattern library: which hooks work for parents on Instagram, which arcs work for educators on LinkedIn. In Phase 2 the Studio will surface this back to the carousel playbook as prior-standard context.
